  [C1.REACTIVATION | 58 words]
  VO: An adiabatic step trades no heat with the outside. The gas is either squeezed too fast for heat to escape, or wrapped so heat cannot flow. With no heat coming in, any work the gas does is paid straight out of its own energy, so its temperature has to move. Compress it and it heats. Let it expand and it cools.
  SCREEN: a small insulated cylinder animates. A compression stroke reddens the gas and a temperature bar rises. An expansion stroke cools it and the bar falls. The word "no heat" sits on the insulation.

  [C1.WORKING RESULTS | 74 words]
  VO: Two results carry this block. First, for an adiabatic change, temperature times volume to the power gamma minus one stays constant. Second, gamma is set by the atoms. A monatomic molecule stores energy in three ways. That makes C-v three halves R and C-p five halves R. So gamma is five thirds, and gamma minus one is two thirds. That two thirds is the exponent you will use.
  SCREEN: result card one, "T times V to the gamma minus one is constant". Result card two, the monatomic values, C-v, C-p, gamma, and gamma minus one equals two thirds. Both cards stay parked for later.

  [C1.EDGE CASES | 89 words | tag TD-ADI]
  VO: Here is where hands slip. The reflex is to treat the gas like a simple isothermal squeeze and write temperature times volume is constant. Watch it break. That would need the exponent to be one, but the true exponent is two thirds, so the plain product is not fixed at all. The guard is simple. On an adiabatic leg the volume always carries the power gamma minus one, never the power one. And a second slip, the exponent goes with temperature and volume together, not with pressure, so keep the pressure form and the temperature form apart.
  SCREEN: the tempting line "T times V is constant" writes itself, then a cross strikes it as the exponent one is shown to be wrong. The correct line restores with the two thirds exponent boxed as the guard card.

  [C2.REACTIVATION | 60 words]
  VO: Isobaric means the pressure is held fixed while the gas breathes. With pressure constant, the ideal gas law ties volume and temperature together, so doubling the volume doubles the temperature. And because the gas expands, it pushes on its surroundings. So the heat you add now does two jobs. It lifts the internal energy, and it does the pushing work.
  SCREEN: a cylinder with a free piston under a constant weight. Adding heat slides the piston out, the volume bar and the temperature bar rise together in lockstep. Two arrows split the incoming heat into an energy arrow and a work arrow.

  [C2.WORKING RESULTS | 74 words]
  VO: Two results again. First, at constant pressure the heat added is n times C-p times the change in temperature. For our monatomic gas C-p is five halves R. Second, volume and temperature move together. The jump from one twenty five to two fifty is a doubling. So the temperature at Y is twice the temperature at X. That gives the temperature change you feed into the heat.
  SCREEN: result card, "heat equals n C-p times change in temperature", with C-p five halves R beside it. Second card, "volume doubles, so temperature doubles", and the T at Y slot fills with twice T at X.

  [C2.EDGE CASES | 82 words | tag TD-CPCV]
  VO: This is the classic slip. Under time pressure a hand reaches for C-v, three halves R, because that is the value everyone memorises first. Watch why it breaks. C-v only counts the energy stored inside the gas. On a constant pressure leg the gas also does pushing work, and C-p is exactly C-v plus that extra R. Use C-v here and you undercount the heat by a full n R times the temperature change. The guard, constant pressure always takes C-p.
  SCREEN: the tempting line with C-v three halves R writes, then a cross strikes it. An arrow shows the missing work term, R, being added back to make C-p five halves R. The guard card "constant pressure takes C-p" pins.
